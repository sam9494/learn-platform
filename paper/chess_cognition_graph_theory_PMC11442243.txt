ABSTRACT
================================================================================

The study examined how chess affects cognition using graph theory analysis. Researchers
compared 19 chess players (mean age 49.5) with 19 matched controls using 27 cognitive
measures across multiple domains. Chess players demonstrated superior visuospatial
abilities and displayed higher local efficiency but lower global efficiency in their
cognitive connectome. Graph analysis revealed executive/attention/processing speed and
visuoconstructive abilities played central roles in chess players' cognitive networks.
The cognitive architecture showed reorganization into three functionally coherent modules
(visual, verbal, and executive/attention/processing speed) compared to the four
less-organized modules observed in controls.

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Chess involves "multiple cognitive processes" including encoding, remembering, and
processing moves through symbolic descriptions. Players organize knowledge of openings,
middlegame themes, and endgame techniques around verbal labels.

Research suggests intensive chess practice positively impacts cognitive performance. Top
chess players demonstrated "superior verbal skills, represented by a high frequency of
professional writing occupations and mastery of foreign languages." Chess practice also
influences perceptual processing -- expert players recognize game plays and recall
positions through perceptual organization and internal position representation, enabling
better decision-making by focusing on piece clusters rather than individual pieces.

Studies comparing chess players versus non-players show mixed results. Some found higher
performance in verbal auditory memory, visuospatial working memory, and planning
abilities. However, other studies found no differences or worse performance in chess
players on certain measures. These inconsistencies may reflect the limited number of
cognitive measures in most studies, failing to comprehensively map cognitive domains.
Additionally, univariate analysis cannot capture the complexity of interactive cognitive
engagement that chess demands. Multivariate approaches like graph theory enable studying
complex associations among multiple cognitive measures.

Recent graph theory research on cognitive measures characterizes the "cognitive
connectome," representing complex organization among multiple measures. However, no
previous study investigated chess players' cognitive connectomes using multivariate
analysis. This approach could clarify benefits of intensive intellectual practice for
brain stimulation and age-related cognitive decline risk reduction, potentially better
reflecting how brains handle complex cognitive demands like chess.

The study aimed to investigate adult chess players' cognitive connectomes through three
approaches: univariate analysis of cognitive performance, multivariate graph theory
analysis comparing integration and segregation measures, and community structure analysis
for understanding cognitive architecture.

================================================================================
2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
================================================================================

2.1. PARTICIPANTS
-----------------

Research included 38 total participants: 19 chess players and 19 controls aged 39-69
years. Chess group recruitment occurred through announcements at the chess federation
and several chess clubs in Tenerife, Spain. Control group selection came from the
GENIC-database (Group of Neuropsychological Studies of the Canary Islands) by matching
controls to chess players on "age, sex, education level, and manual preference using the
Edinburgh Handedness Inventory". All participants were male by study design. Both study
groups reported leisure and physical activities, with controls not playing chess regularly
or fulfilling chess group inclusion criteria. Crystallized intelligence assessment
utilized the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS-III) Information subtest.

Inclusion criteria specified: "(1) normal cognitive performance in comprehensive
neuropsychological assessment using age-, sex-, and education-adjusted normative data
(2) no neurologic, psychiatric, or systemic diseases with a potential impact on cognitive
performance; and (3) no history of substance abuse." The study received ethics approval
from the University of La Laguna (Spain), project number 57, with all participants
providing written informed consent per the Declaration of Helsinki.

2.3. NETWORK CONSTRUCTION AND GRAPH ANALYSIS
----------------------------------------------

The cognitive variables serving as network nodes are listed in Table 2. "No cognitive
variables from the extensive neuropsychological protocol were excluded for the graph
analysis." Prior to network construction, processing speed variables underwent inversion
so higher scores reflected better performance universally. Edges derived from
group-specific Pearson correlation coefficient association matrices between node pairs.

The resulting correlation matrices underwent binarization through correlation coefficient
thresholding "at a range of network densities (min = 10% to max = 45%, in steps of 1%),
to ensure fully connected networks and exclude densities where connectomes had random
topologies". Self-connections and negative correlations were excluded.

Calculated measures included global metrics: "average global efficiency, average local
efficiency, and transitivity measures calculated from the binary networks across the
different densities, and the average strength calculated from the weighted network".
Nodal measures comprised nodal global efficiency, nodal local efficiency, and nodal
closeness centrality. Modular analyses employed "the Louvain algorithm" on weighted
undirected networks with gamma value of 1.


2.4. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS
--------------------------

Analysis utilized R programming environment and BRAPH software. Data were assessed for
outliers; distribution normality confirmed through histogram inspection and
asymmetry/kurtosis computation. Mann-Whitney U tests analyzed demographic variables.

Univariate analysis compared chess players versus controls across 27 cognitive variables
using t-tests for continuous variables and Mann-Whitney U tests for non-normally
distributed variables. Effect sizes employed Cohen's d or Hedge's g, with significance
at p <= 0.05 (two-tailed).

Global graph measure comparisons occurred through "1,000 nonparametric permutations over
the previously pre-specified range of network densities (min = 10% to max = 45%, in
steps of 1%)." Nodal analyses employed "the 10-45% range of network densities, and
results were reported at the median density (27.5%)". False discovery rate (FDR) method
adjusted p-values for multiple testing in nodal analysis. Community structure differences
received inspection between groups.

================================================================================
3. RESULTS
================================================================================

3.1. FIRST AIM: UNIVARIATE ANALYSIS ACROSS COGNITIVE MEASURES
---------------------------------------------------------------

Demographic characteristics appear in Table 1, with both groups statistically comparable
in crystallized intelligence via the WAIS-III Information subtest.

Chess players outperformed controls in "JLOT - Second half (p = 0.039), with a medium
effect size (g = 0.75)." No other statistically significant differences emerged across
27 cognitive variables. However, visual inspection revealed moderate effect sizes
favoring chess players in STROOP Inhibition (d = 0.56), spatial span forward (g = 0.56),
and visual discrimination VR (g = 0.55), while semantic fluency showed an effect
favoring controls (d = -0.55).


3.2. SECOND AIM: GRAPH ANALYSIS ON THE COGNITIVE CONNECTOME
-------------------------------------------------------------

Weighted correlation matrices revealed chess players displayed "a more widespread pattern
of correlations than controls." Executive measures correlated strongly with premotor
function, visuospatial ability, visuoconstructive ability, visual memory, and naming --
clustering patterns less salient in controls. Verbal memory measures showed stronger
correlations with executive function, semantic fluency, and visual memory recognition in
chess players versus controls.

Global graph measures demonstrated chess players had "a higher average local efficiency
and a lower average global efficiency, compared with the control group." No statistically
significant differences emerged in transitivity or average strength.

Nodal analysis revealed chess players showed higher nodal closeness centrality in
"STROOP Words, STROOP Colors, STROOP inhibition, VR Copying, VR Total Recognition, and
Luria's hand alternating movements (HAM) with the right hand." Controls showed higher
closeness centrality in FAS. No significant differences appeared in nodal global or
local efficiency.

NODAL GRAPH ANALYSIS SUMMARY (Table 4):

Chess players showed significantly higher closeness centrality in:
  - Stroop Words
  - Stroop Colors
  - Stroop Inhibition
  - VR Copying
  - VR Total Recognition
  - Luria's Hand Alternating Movements (Right)

Controls showed higher centrality only in:
  - Phonemic fluency (FAS)


3.3. THIRD AIM: ARCHITECTURE OF COGNITIVE CONNECTOME THROUGH MODULAR ANALYSIS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Chess players displayed three modules versus four in controls. Chess players' architecture
showed "three modules...an executive/processing speed module (yellow circle), a visual
module including visual memory, visuoconstructive, visuoperceptive, and visuospatial
measures (orange circle), and a verbal memory module (blue circle)." Controls'
organization was less coherent, mixing cognitive functions across modules.

================================================================================
4. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

The study investigated the cognitive connectome of adult chess players using graph theory
alongside univariate analyses. Results indicated chess players possessed "a more
integrated connectome locally, where nodes related to executive/attention/processing
speed and visuoconstructive abilities played a central role."

Univariate findings demonstrated chess players performed better in visuospatial abilities.
The authors noted "High performance in visuospatial abilities in chess players was also
reported in a previous study." While other cognitive domains showed large effect sizes
favoring chess players, statistical significance wasn't achieved. The researchers
suggested "those differences also existed in our cohort but were difficult to capture
due to our slightly smaller sample size" or because most previous studies included
younger participants.

Graph theory expanded previous univariate research by revealing chess players demonstrated
increased between-module and within-module connectivity. The authors explained this
pattern likely resulted from "the high cognitive demand of intensive chess practice over
time." Visual inspection and modular analysis supported cognitive connectome
reorganization in chess players.

Quantitative global graph analysis confirmed chess players exhibited higher average local
efficiency. The authors noted this "partly aligns" with previous neuroimaging findings
showing chess experts displayed "a higher clustering coefficient than novice players."

Regarding lower global efficiency in chess players, the researchers proposed this
reflected specialization: "The intensive training of a visual subnetwork and the high
demands on an executive/attention/processing speed subnetwork...may rewire the
connectome of chess players into a highly specialized connectome." This specialization
came "at the cost of a lower capacity to integrate the connectome globally."

The authors acknowledged several limitations: the sample consisted entirely of males due
to "low representation of women chess players"; the relatively small sample size, though
"similar or exceeds the sample size of some previous chess studies"; lack of analysis
regarding expertise levels; and the cross-sectional design preventing causal inference
establishment.

================================================================================
5. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

The researchers conclude: "The cognitive architecture of chess players is slightly
reorganized into functionally and anatomically coherent modules reflecting a distinction
between visual, verbal, and executive/attention/processing speed related functions,
perhaps reminiscent of right hemisphere and left hemisphere subnetworks orchestrated by
the frontal lobe and its white matter connections."
